Long lost where no pathway goes

原歌词：

My star, I saw it high and far
At the parting of the ways
A light on the edge of the outer night
Like silver set ablaze
Where the round world plunges steeply down
But on the old roads goes
As an unseen bridge that on arches runs
To coasts that no man knows

I would not find the burning domes and sands
Where reigns the sun, nor dare the snows
Nor seek in mountains dark the landscapes of the men
Long lost to where no pathway goes

But where they bloom those flowers fair
In what air or land they grow
What words beyond the world I heard
If you would seek for know

In a boat then, brother, far afloat
You must labour in the sea
And find yourself things out of mind:
You will learn no more of me

I would not find the burning domes and sands
Where reigns the sun, nor dare the deadly snows
Nor seek in mountains dark the landscapes of the men
Of long lost to where no pathway goes

There the twain enfolded,
Phantom twilight, phantom twilight,
And dim the mazes dark, unholy.

I would never find the burning domes and sands
Where reigns the sun, nor dare the snows
Nor seek in mountains dark, the landscapes of the men
Long lost to whom no pathway goes

副歌部分歌词来自《失落的传说》第一章《嬉乐不再的小屋》中收录的诗歌，Trees of Kortirion: IV - Mettanyë。以下为原文，加粗部分为引用片段。

Mettanyë
I would not find the burning domes and sands
Where reigns the sun, nor dare the deadly snows,
Nor seek in mountains dark the hidden lands
Of men long lost to whom no pathway goes;
I heed no call of clamant bell that rings
Iron-tongued in the towers of earthly kings.
Here on the stones and trees there lies a spell
Of unforgotten loss, of memory more blest
Than mortal wealth. Here undefeated dwell
The Folk Immortal under withered elms,
Alalminórë once in ancient realms.

这里先讲讲整首诗的由来：

Trees of Kortirion由J.R.R. Tolkien本人最早于1915年11月创作，存在三个不同的版本，托老在1937年对这首诗进行了较大的修改， 而在距1915年近半个世纪后托老发表了Trees of Kortirion的最终版本。

对于整首诗的翻译这里就不献丑了，有需要的还是自己拿软件翻译一下，这里主要说下里面的一些名词。

Kortirion，即科尔提力安，由伊理安之子英格威以及在大迁徙中追随他前往托尔艾瑞西亚岛的精灵共同建立。英格威将此城称为Koromas，意为流亡者于科尔(Kôr)的休息处，并在此处建立了一座巨大的塔。之后Koromas改名为Kortirion，昆雅语，由Kôr(曾经是诺多族在维林诺上的首都) + tirion((雄大的)塔;山丘上的城市)组成。嬉乐不再的小屋就坐落在科尔提力安中，这也是水手埃里欧尔到达的地方。


科尔提力安的纹章

Mettanyë中的metta意为结束、终结，与诗中反复出现的再也找不到一起理解应该是暗示了科尔提力安最后的命运，虽为永生的精灵所建立，但也无法逃脱如同刚多林一样陨落。

Alalminórë处于托尔艾瑞西亚岛的中心地带，是整个岛最平整的地区，因此有很多精灵建立的城镇与村庄，当然科尔提力安也是其中之一。Alalminórë在昆雅语中意思是“榆树之地”，由alalmë(榆树) + nórë(土地)组成。其灵感来源是英格兰的沃里克郡，而该地区的沃里克镇也是科尔提力安的原型。

倒数第二段来自《中洲历史》第三卷《贝烈瑞安德的歌谣》中的第一章节《胡林的子女之歌》。加粗部分为引用片段。

“Thus reached they roots and the ruinous feet of those hoary hills that Hithlum girdle, the shaggy pinewoods
of the Shadowy Mountains. There the twain enfolded phantom twilight and dim mazes dark, unholy, in Nan Dungorthin where
nameless gods have shrouded shrines in shadows secret, more old than Morgoth or the ancient lords the golden Gods of the guarded West”

这里的主角为胡林之子图林·图伦拔，是第一纪元中最具悲剧色彩的英雄，相关事迹可以在《精灵宝钻》中自行阅读。此片段选自于图林误杀贝烈格后，与Flinding(格温多早期版本的名字)一同穿过希斯路姆前往Flinding的故乡——纳国斯隆德，在途径Nan Dungortheb(恐怖死亡山谷)时遇到不洁幻影。诗中提到Nan Dungortheb中曾存在着无名的神灵，甚至比魔苟斯更加古老，这里的神灵指的是乌苟立安特(Ungoliant)。

乌苟立安特是一个有着蜘蛛外形的恶灵，其起源笼罩在神秘之中，有人认为她是一位魔苟斯曾经腐化的迈雅，但她从未被列入已知的爱努之中；也有人说她源自阿尔达以外的虚空。她在双树纪元与魔苟斯一起潜入维林诺，吸干了双圣树的汁液，终结了双树纪元，促使众维拉后来创造了太阳与月亮。之后乌苟立安特逃往Nan Dungortheb，孕育了新的黑暗和恐怖，使此地变得不祥。最后她前往了被遗忘的南方世界，再无记载，相传她在饥饿到极点后吞噬了自己。

主歌部分取自 Imram，别名《圣布兰登之死》， 是J.R.R. Tolkien创作的诗歌，在1945年收录在《概念社文集》中，以Philip Frankley的口吻撰写，之后托老于1955年12月3日发表在《Time and Tide》杂志上。Imram 在爱尔兰语中意为航行。现实中，Immrama在凯尔特传说里指爱尔兰探险家在大西洋寻找允诺之地这类故事。

我这里把整首诗的梗概写一下，如有错误恳请指正：

在圣布兰登临终之际，一位门徒问及他的西行之旅。而他除了一朵云、一棵树和一颗星以外，所见所闻都已淡忘。当时布兰登与同伴一起航行了一年多，一朵乌云在他们头上展开，遮天蔽日分不清白天黑夜，而将大海与乌云连接的是一座陡峭的高山。他们继续航行直到把云甩在身后，到达了一个星光灿烂的海滩。上岸并穿过一个寂静的山谷后，他们发现了一颗树，无人能丈量它的高度，纯白的树叶如同鹅毛般狭长柔软。这梦幻的场景让一行人为不能在此驻留而难过，悲伤中他们开始轻轻歌唱。突然树开始抖动，落叶纷飞，从璀璨的星空之上他们听到一首歌，不是鸟鸣，不似人声，也不是天使的声音，也许是人类的远亲仍在这个世界上徘徊。门徒又问到那颗星的事，他说那是走在一条位于世界之上的古桥时看见了它。门徒坚持想知道他在生命之地看到了什么，布兰登却答道，“那就上船吧，兄弟，向着远方航行/你必须劳作于大海之上/为你自己寻找心中的事物:/你不会再了解我了。”然后圣布兰登死去，航向没有归途的旅程，葬在爱尔兰。

以下提供 Imram 的原文，加粗部分为引用片段。

Part I
At last out of the deep sea he passed,
and mist rolled on the shore;
under clouded moon the waves were loud
as the laden ship him bore
to Ireland, back to wood and mire
and the tower tall and grey,
where the knell of Clain-ferta’s bell
tolled in green Galway
Where Shannon down to Lough Derg ran
under a rain-clad sky
Saint Brenand came to his journey’s end
to find the grace to die.
“O tell me, father, for I loved you well,
if still you have words for me,
of things strange in the remembering
in the long and lonely sea,
of islands by deep spell beguiled,
where dwell the Elvenking:
in seven long years the road to Heaven
or the Living Land did you find?”
“The things I have seen, the many things,
have long now faded far;
only three come clear now back to me:
a Cloud, a Tree, a Star.
“We sailed for a year and a day and hailed
no field nor coast of men;
no boat nor bird saw we ever afloat
for forty days and ten.
Then a drumming we heard as of thunder coming
and a Cloud above us spread;
we saw no sun at set or dawn
yet ever the west was red.
“Upread from sea to cloud then sheer
a shoreless mountain stood;
its side were black from the sullen tide
up to its smoking hood,
but its spire was lit with a living fire
that ever rose and fell:
tall as a column in High Heaven’s hall,
its roots were deep as Hell;
Grounded in chasms the waters drowned
and swallowed long ago
it stand, I guess, on the foundered land
where te kings of kings live low.
“We sailed then on till all winds failed,
and we toiled then with the oar;
we burned with thirst and in hunger yearned,
and we sange our psalms no more.
As last beyond the Cloud we passed
and came to a starlit strand
the wave were sighing in pillared caves,
grinding gems to sand.
And here they would grind our bones we feared
until the end of time;
for steep those shores went upward leaping
to cliffs no man could climb.
But round by west a firth we found
that clove the mountain-wall;
there lay a water shadow-grey
between the mountains tall
Through gates of stone we rowed in haste,
and passed, and left the sea;
and silence like dew fell in that isle,
and holy it seemed to be.
“To a dale we came like a silver grail
with carven hills for rim.
In that hidden land we saw there stand
under moonlight dim
a Tree more fair that ever I deemed
in Paradise might grow:
its foot was like a great tower’s root,
its height no man could know;
and white as winter to my sight
the leave of that Tree were;
they grew more close than swan-wing plumes,
long and soft and fair.
“It seemed to us then as in a drem
that time had passed away,
and our journey ended; for no return
we hoped, but there to stay.
In the silence of that hollow isle
half sadly then we sang:
softly we thought, but the sound aloft
like sudden trumpets rang.
The Tree then shook, and flying free
from its limbs the leaves in air
as white birds rose in weeling flight,
and the lifting boughs were bare.
In high we heard in the starlit sky
a song, but not of bird:
neither noise of man nor angels voice,
but maybe is a third
fair kindred in the world yet lingers
beyond the foundered land
But steep are the seas and the waters deep
beyond the White-tree Strand!”

Part II
“O stay now, father!, There is more to say.
But two things you have told:
the Tree, the Cloud,; but you spoke of three.
The Star in mind do you hold?”
“The Star? Why, I saw it high and far
at the parting of the ways,
a light on the edge of the Outer Night
beyond the Door of Days,
where the round world plunges steeply down,
but on the old road goes
as an unseen bridge that on arches runs
to coasts that no man knows”
“But men say, father, that ere the end
you went where none have been.
I would hear you tell me, father dear,
of last land you have seen”
“In my mind the Star I Still cand find,
and the parting of the seas,
and the breath as sweet and keen as death
that was borne upon the breeze.
But where they bloom, those flowers fair,
in what air or land they grow,
what words beyond this world I heard,
if you would seek to know,
in a boat then, brother, far afloat
you must labour in the sea,
and find for yourself things out of mind:
you will learn no more of me”
In Ireland over wood andmire
in the tower tall and grey
the Knell of Clain-ferta’s bell
was tolling in green Galway.
Saint Brendan had come to his life’s end
under a rain-clad sky,
journeying whence no ship returns;
and his bones in Ireland lie.
